Degradation and Outages

SAM's performance model calculates the system's hourly our subhourly electricity output for a single year. You can use the curtailment and availability factors and degradation rate described below to model system outages or a year-to-year decline in the system's output.

Note. SAM allows you to specify curtailment and availability factors greater than one, and negative degradation rates, so you can use the factors to adjust the system output upward or downward. You can also specify negative values for the curtailment and availability factors, although you should avoid doing so unless you have a reason to model negative electrical output.

Curtailment and Availability

The curtailment and availability factors allow you to adjust the annual our hourly output values that SAM's performance model calculates. The factors make it possible to model reductions in the system's output due to maintenance down times (availability), system shutdowns required by the grid operator (curtailment), or any other reason that might  cause the energy delivered to the grid to be different than predicted by the model.

Here are some examples of applications of the curtailment and availability factors:

•Unscheduled maintenance results in a 96% annual availability factor for the system: Specify an annual factor of 0.96.
•The system is scheduled to be completely shut down for maintenance on October 1 through 7 between 8 am and noon: Use hourly factors with custom periods to specify a start time of October 1 at 12 a.m., end time of October 7 at 11 p.m., and a factor of 0.

Degradation

The degradation rate is a year-to-year reduction in the system's AC electrical output. The following are examples of applications for the degradation rate:

•The module output in a photovoltaic system is expected to decrease at an average rate of 0.5% over the 30-year life of the system. Use a degradation rate of 0.5% to estimate the financial impact of the reduction. Note that in an actual system, the output reduction is in the array's DC output rather than the system's AC output, so this is only a rough estimate of the effect of photovoltaic module degradation on the system's output.
•A wind project is in a region with transmission constraints that the project planners expect will cause curtailment of 5% of the system's annual electrical output starting in Year six of the project life: Use degradation in Schedule mode, and specify a degradation rate of 0 for Years one through five, and 5% Years six and later.
•The inverter in a photovoltaic system is replaced in Year 15 with a 5% more efficient model than was originally installed in Year 0: Use degradation in Schedule mode, and specify a degradation rate of 0 for Years one through 14, and 105% for Years 15 and later.

Note. For the solar water heating model, the performance model calculates the electrical energy saved by the system rather than electricity generated by the system.
